Daniel Defoe

"the father of the English novel"
(1660-1731)

His life

Early life

  • born into a middle-class family of Dissenters;
  • received education in Dissenting academy;
  • studied practical subjects (geography, modern languages and economy).

Adult life

  1. business:

    he went bankrupt twice as a merchant and got imprisoned for his debts;

  2. journalism:

    he wrote in Whig papers and published periodically The Review;

  3. intellectual

    he gained fame for his political essays and pamphlets.

In 1703, he got arrested for his writings under the reign of Queen Anne, who was against Nonconformists.
He is condemned to the pillory, but he is acclaimed by the people.
He then denies his Whig ideas for freedom and starts writing for the Tories.

Writing novels

Despite starting only when he was 59 years old, Defoe would become most famous for his novels. He then managed to live comfortably but died in 1731 while still being heavily in debt.

Defoe's novels

  • Great production (unrevised novels);
  • spokesman of a new social class;
  • realistic novel;
  • didactic aim (moral teachings).

Defoe's main character

Defoe's novels are fictional autobiographies: the main character is the common thread among an otherwise scattered series of episodes.

The narration is in 1st person and retrospective. The character is often in a condition of isolation and hardship and carries out a spiritual journey.

Defoe also provides an in-depth look into the character's psychology through their actions rather than by the outside.

I was born in the year 1632, in the city of York, of a good family, tho' not of that country, my father being a foreigner of Bremen, who settled first at Hull. He got a good estate by merchandise, and leaving off his trade, lived afterwards at York, from whence he had married my mother, whose relations were named Robinson, a very good family in that country, and from whom I was called Robinson Kreutznaer; but, by the usual corruption of words in England, we are now called, nay, we call our selves, and write our name, Crusoe, and so my companions always called me.
